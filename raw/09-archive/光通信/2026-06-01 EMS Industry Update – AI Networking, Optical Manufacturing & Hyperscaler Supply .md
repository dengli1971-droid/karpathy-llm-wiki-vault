# EMS Industry Update – AI Networking, Optical Manufacturing & Hyperscaler Supply Chain Shifts

**日期**: 2026-06-01 | **来源**: forum.thirdbridge.com | **公司**: AWS, Amazon, Apple, Broadcom, Coherent, Dell, Google, HPE, Intel, Lumentum, Meta, Microsoft, Supermicro, TSMC | **标签**: Scaling Law | **Ticker**: 2330 TT, AAPL US, AMZN US, AVGO US, COHR US, DELL US, GOOGL US, HPE US, INTC US, LITE US, META US, MSFT US, TSM US

## 核心观点
- AI infrastructure is shifting value creation from individual servers towards integrated rack-level systems, requiring compute, networking, storage, power and cooling to be designed, tested and deployed as a unified architecture
- Hyperscalers are becoming more dependent on a small group of strategic manufacturing partners, creating longer-term relationships centred on engineering expertise, process capability and roadmap alignment
- ODMs appear better-positioned for the current AI infrastructure build-out given their longstanding server-design expertise, though Flex, Jabil and other EMS providers are investing aggressively to close the gap through rack-level integration, liquid cooling and systems capabilities
- Fabrinet's competitive position appears rooted in specialised optical manufacturing processes, with expert estimating competitors could require billions of dollars and years to replicate its capabilities
- CPOs could materially reshape the optical ecosystem by reducing reliance on pluggable transceivers, potentially benefiting advanced optical manufacturers while challenging vendors concentrated in traditional pluggable architectures

## 问答
### Q1: AI infrastructure demand has become a major growth driver across major EMS [electronic manufacturing services] players such as Celestica, Jabil and Flex. How do you think AI has changed what hyperscalers want from manufacturing partners compared to prior cloud build cycles?

At the highest level, enterprise racks were like Lego building blocks where you had the rack and you bought a server from Dell and you plugged it in, and maybe that rack took up to 8kW. Then telecom side, you had the telecom racks, the telco racks. It was really plug and play, and you can mix your 1U server, your 2U server, you can mix vendors. It came down to the software and the management software you're using. As you move forward in time into what we consider like cloud deployments where you got custom silicon, and custom hardware that's coming out of the hyperscalers. They've moved to larger-sized racks, 48U, 52U. They've moved to a lot more power, 25kW, 50kW, 66kW, 120kW, 200kW and moved to new thermal solutions like liquid cooling and changing… The rack itself has become more integral into the data centre design. The whole data centre is now being architected for AI workloads vs this, "I could buy any server from anybody model." That has changed the relationship with the EMS vendors because now they have to be able to deliver a rack-level solution with the power, the cooling, the compute, the networking and the storage, all integrated in a holistic whole.

### Q2: EMS providers were viewed as interchangeable manufacturing partners. Do you think certain manufacturers are becoming more strategically embedded within the customer roadmaps as a result of the changes that you mentioned?

Yes, because it's very difficult when you're deploying millions of servers based on millions of components across the global supply chain to quickly change vendors. If you think of the old EMS model where you just received an RFQ, you won the RFQ, you executed on the forecast and the build for 2-3 years, the RFQ ended, and you went out and bid on the next one. This is much more multigenerational. They have three or four partners that they've identified and then they split the volume and percentages across those partners. The partners are trained to work a certain way. Even the partners now are working together where one might provide one part of the solution and one might provide another part of the solution. This whole thing has to come together seamlessly into the data centre as the racks are delivered. I would say that the partners are more integrated now than a traditional EMS model. The other thing I would say is the hyperscalers are expecting the partners to bring more to the table. Instead of them bringing a test and saying, "Here, run this test, give me the number," they show them a problem and they say, "How would you test this? What would you do to optimise this? What would you do to lower the cost of this or increase the performance?" They're expecting to be more part of the solution and have expertise that they're bringing to the table.

### Q3: Across the EMS landscape, which companies appear best-positioned to capture the incremental AI spend that we're seeing? What differentiates the leaders from the rest of the field? Is it the ability to prove those testing capabilities that we were just talking about?

Industry analysts like IDC and Gartner have come up with this term in the past couple of years called ODM Direct, original design manufacturer direct, where the hyperscalers are bypassing the OEMs. They're not going to Dell, HPE, IBM, who work with the EMS and the ODMs, and then going around them, going around the value-added resellers, straight to the manufacturer. ODMs like a Quanta, a Wistron, an Inventec, a Foxconn, were well positioned and have been embedded in that relationship for years because they have portfolios of servers that they can easily then transition that knowledge to custom design servers based on custom design silicon. Where the EMS comes in like a Flex or a Jabil is maybe the manufacturing services where you take those pre-existing parts from another vendor that has expertise in server design, but then your expertise is global supply chain, operations, global footprint. The winners are going to be the ones that can combine those two things together.

### Q41: There's been growing industry discussion around CPOs [co-packaged optics] and tighter integration between optics and compute. Can you explain what CPOs are? How significant could these architectural shifts ultimately become?

Short answer is yes. If you think about networking in general, there's a physical interface on one side, where you plug in the cable. In the case of ethernet, you've got an ethernet cable with a little weird head and you plug it into the ethernet port. Then you've got the MAC on the other side, the media access controller. Then in there, you've got a DSP and memory and a little compute engine, you've got all kind of electronics. You've got a mechanical. If you think about a network card, you've got the card itself that plugs into a slot, you've got some power modules, you've got some memory, you've got some little chips that hold your software that's coded in there. You've got a lot of stuff that adds heat, that adds size, that adds complexity. Now, do the same thing with an optical pluggable module. You've got a little rectangular module. It's got an interface on one side, it's got DSPs and PCBAs and other things on the inside. If I take all that electronics like the DSP and I put it co-packaged with the processor silicon. Now I've got…

Manufacturing now has gone away from monolithic processor manufacturing to begin with because of the size and complexity. Now, I have chiplets and I can mix and match. I can have a CPU chiplet, a GPU chiplet, a memory chiplet and I can mix and match and build my ASIC how I want to. I'm making this up. I've got the DSP built into the processor on an interposer. Now, I have gotten rid of the inside half of the pluggable module. Then I run the fibre cable, the fibre optic straight into this interposer and I've gotten rid of the external half. I've lowered power, I've increased performance, I've lowered the size, I've increased density, but I've just massively increased the manufacturing and process complexity.

### Q42: How significant do you think that architectural shift is ultimately going to become?

I think along with that and CXL, I think this stands for compute express link. I keep wanting to say COM, and I know it's not COM.
